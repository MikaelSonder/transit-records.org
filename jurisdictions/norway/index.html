Executive Summary

Customs clearance in Norway is the structured professional function through which goods entering or leaving the jurisdiction are presented, documented, declared and processed for lawful border movement. As a rule, everyone bringing goods into Norway must tell Norwegian Customs at the border what they are bringing in and how much, and goods must generally be declared upon import. [page:1]

Operationally, customs clearance in Norway often begins before the goods arrive at the border. Norwegian Customs explains that imported goods commonly arrive with a shipping agent, are placed in a customs storage facility and must be cleared before retrieval for use. [page:1]

The Norwegian system is shaped by national customs administration, electronic declaration practice and importer responsibility. Norwegian Customs states that forwarding agents often obtain customs clearance on behalf of importers through the electronic customs clearance system TVINN, but the importer remains responsible for ensuring compliance with the regulations. [page:1]

Cross-border relevance is substantial because Norwegian customs clearance frequently affects foreign suppliers, non-resident traders, Nordic distribution chains, e-commerce flows and international documentation standards. For many businesses, Norway is a distinct non-EU customs destination requiring separate process planning. [page:1][web:21]

Purpose

The purpose of the customs clearance function is to ensure that goods moving into or out of Norway are processed through the relevant customs framework in a controlled, documented and legally coherent manner.

It exists to reduce border disruption, improve declaration accuracy, support lawful release of goods and align commercial trade activity with the customs procedures that apply to the shipment, the goods and the route involved.
